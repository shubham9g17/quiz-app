## Chandragupta I
- He was a prominent ruler of ancient India and is regarded as the real founder of the Gupta Empire. Though his grandfather Sri Gupta established the dynasty, Chandragupta I was the first to assume the imperial title 'Maharajadhiraja' (King of Kings), marking the beginning of Gupta political dominance.
- He came to power around 320 CE and significantly expanded the kingdom through strategic alliances, most notably his marriage to Kumaradevi, a princess of the powerful Lichchhavi clan. This alliance helped him gain control over important regions in North India, particularly in present-day Uttar Pradesh.
- He regarded the foundation for the empire's golden age, which reached its height under his son, Samudragupta.

## Gupta Dynasty – Genealogy & Achievements
- **Samudragupta's Lineage:** Father: Chandragupta I; Mother: Kumara Devi from the Lichchhavi gan
- **Chandragupta II (Vikramaditya):** Son of Samudragupta; Known from inscriptions and coins; Defeated the Shakas in western India
- **Royal Titles:** Chandragupta I was the first Gupta ruler to assume Maharajadhiraja ('Great King of Kings'); Samudragupta continued using this grand title; Earlier rulers (great grandfather & grandfather) used only the title Maharaja
- **Vikram Samvat Era:** Began in 58 BCE; Commemorates Chandragupta II's victory over the Shakas

## Samudragupta
- Samudragupta was a very famous ruler of the Gupta dynasty. We know about Samudragupta from a long inscription, inscribed on the Ashokan pillar at Allahabad.
- Harishena inscribed a long poem on Samudragupta on the Ashokan pillar at Allahabad. He was the court poet of Samudragupta. This inscription is of a special kind known as a prashasti, a Sanskrit word, meaning 'in praise of'.
- Harishena praised the king in glowing terms—as a warrior and as a king who was learned. He also described him as equal to the gods.
- Harishena described four different kinds of rulers and wrote about Samudragupta's policies towards them:
  1. Aryavarta, where nine rulers were uprooted and their kingdoms were made a part of Samudragupta's empire.
  2. Dakshinapatha, where twelve rulers surrendered to Samudragupta after being defeated, and he later allowed them to rule again.
  3. The inner circle of neighbouring states, including Assam, coastal Bengal, Nepal and a number of gana sanghas in the northwest. They brought tribute, followed his orders and attended his court.
  4. The rulers of the outlying areas, perhaps the descendants of the Kushanas and Shakas and the ruler of Sri Lanka submitted to him and offered daughters in marriage.

## Chandragupta II
- He was also known as Vikramaditya, was one of the greatest rulers of the Gupta Empire. He ruled from around 380 CE to 415 CE and is remembered for his military conquests, cultural achievements, and promotion of art and learning.
- He expanded the empire by defeating the powerful Shaka (Western Kshatrapas) rulers in western India, gaining control over regions like Malwa, Gujarat, and Saurashtra. This gave the Guptas access to important trade routes and ports, boosting the economy.
- Chandragupta II's court was famous for its scholars and poets, including the legendary Kalidasa. His reign is considered the high point of the Gupta Golden Age, marked by peace, prosperity, and remarkable achievements in science, literature, and art.

## The Pallavas, Chalukyas and Pulakeshin's Prashasti
- The Pallavas and Chalukyas were the most important ruling dynasties in south India during this period.
- The kingdom of the Pallavas spread from the region around their capital, Kanchipuram, to the Kaveri delta, while that of the Chalukyas was centred around the Raichur Doab, between the rivers Krishna and Tungabhadra.
- Aihole, the capital of the Chalukyas, was an important trading centre. It developed as a religious centre, with a number of temples.
- Pulakeshin II was the best-known Chalukya ruler. His prashasti was composed by his court poet Ravikirti, which talks about his ancestors, who are traced back through four generations from father to son.

## How Were These Kingdoms Administered?
- Land revenue remained important for these rulers, and the village remained the basic unit of administration.
- The latest development witnessed in the process of administration was the adoption of new methods by the king to gain the support of the rich and powerful in terms of economic, social and political conditions:
  1. Some administrative posts were hereditary, for example, Harishena was the maha-danda-nayaka (chief judicial officer) like his father.
  2. Sometimes, one person held many offices. For instance, besides being a maha-danda-nayaka, Harishena was a kumar-amatya (important minister).
  3. Besides this, important men probably had a say in local administration. These included the naga-ra-shreshthi (chief banker), sarthavaha (leader of the merchant caravans), prathama-kulika (chief craftsman), etc.

## A New Kind of Army
- Kings maintained a well-organised army, with elephants, chariots, cavalry and foot soldiers.
- Military leaders provided kings with troops whenever he needed them but they were not paid regular salaries.
- Instead, of salary, some of them received grants of land. They collected revenue from the land and used this to maintain soldiers and horses and provide equipment for warfare. These men were known as samantas.

## Assemblies in the Southern Kingdoms
- **Sabha:** An assembly of Brahmin landowners; Functioned through subcommittees; Irrigation; Agricultural operations; Road construction; Local temple maintenance
- **Nagaram:** Merchant organisation in primarily urban areas; Controlled by rich landowners and merchants
- **Ur:** Village assembly in areas without Brahmin landowners
- **Ordinary People in the Kingdom:** Worked as farmers, labourers, artisans and traders; Paid taxes, performed forced labour (vishti), as provided services to the king; Had limited political power, but were crucial to the economic foundation of the kingdoms

- Very little information has been gathered concerning the existence of ordinary individuals. Different records of history give a brief look at their lives through plays.
- Kalidasa was known for his plays depicting life in the king's court. Interesting features about these plays were:
  1. The king and most Brahmins are shown as speaking Sanskrit.
  2. Women and men other than the king and Brahmins use Prakrit.
- Abhijnana Shakuntalam is the story of the love between a king named Dushyanta and a young woman named Shakuntala.
- The Chinese pilgrim Fa Xian noticed the plight of those who were treated as untouchables by the high and mighty. They were expected to live on the outskirts of the city.

## The King's Army
- The king travelled with an enormous amount of equipment. Apart from weapons, there were things of daily use such as pots, pans, furniture, food, animals, vegetables and spices, carried on carts or loaded on to pack animals such as camels and elephants.
- This huge army was accompanied by musicians beating drums and others playing horns and trumpets. Villagers had to provide hospitality along the way.
- They came with gifts of curds, gur and flowers and provided fodder for the animals. They also tried to meet the king and place their complaints and petitions before him.
