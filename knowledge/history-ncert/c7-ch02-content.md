## Introduction
- Around 2000 BCE, the Indus or Harappan civilisation, known as India's **first urban civilisation**, began to break down. Many of its cities were abandoned. Although certain people survived at some places, they returned to a simple village life.
- [Timeline: Harappan Civilisation 1900 BCE → 1200 BCE → 800 BCE → 400 BCE Janapadas]
- This happened because the key features of urban life had disappeared — big buildings, busy streets, markets, skilled workers like potters and metalworkers, written language, sanitation, proper administration, and a ruling system.
- For approximately 1,000 years after this, **urban life almost vanished** from India, except for a few small towns that may have existed in the north.
- After the Indus cities declined, many regional cultures developed, but we won't go into their details here.
- Later, during the **1st millennium BCE**, a new phase of urbanisation began in the **Ganga plains**, parts of the Indus region, and nearby areas. This spread slowly to other parts of India.
- This is known from two primary sources:
  1. **Archaeological finds** (showing ancient cities), and
  2. **Old texts** like Vedic, Buddhist, and Jain literature, which mention these cities.
- This period is referred to as **India's Second Urbanisation** — and it started a city-based life that continues till today.

## Janapadas and Mahajanpadas
- By the end of the **2nd millennium BCE**, people in North India began forming **clans** with shared language and customs.
- Each clan settled in a region called a **janapada** (meaning "place where people have settled") and was ruled by a **raja (king)**.
- As trade grew, janapadas became more connected and some joined together. By the **8th or 7th century BCE**, larger kingdoms called **mahajanapadas** were formed.
- [Figure: Mahajanpadas map showing locations including Kamboja, Gandhara, Kuru, Panchala, Matsya, Surasena, Avanti, Chedi, Vatsa, Kashi, Kosala, Magadha, Anga, Malla, Vajji, Ashmaka]
- Ancient texts often list **16 mahajanapadas**, from Gandhara in the northwest to Anga in the east and Ashmaka in Central India. Some smaller janapadas still remained independent.
- Most of these ancient cities were **large and well-protected**, with **walls and moats** around them. The gates were narrow and guards kept a check on who entered or left.
- Interestingly, many of these old capitals are still alive today as modern cities—some over **2,500 years old**.

## Early Democratic Traditions
- Each janapada had a **council or assembly** called **sabha** or **samiti**, where important matters were discussed. These words first appear in the **Vedas**.
- The members were mostly **elders**, and the **raja (king)** had to heed their advice. He was **not supposed to rule alone**.
- Certain texts state that a **bad king could be removed** by the assembly — although this may not have been a strict rule, as our information from that time is limited.
- In the mahajanapadas, the political system grew from that of the janapadas.
- Most were **monarchies** — the raja (king) had full power, helped by **ministers and elders**. Kingship was usually **hereditary** (passed from father to son).
- The king collected **taxes**, maintained **law and order**, built **forts**, and kept an **army**. Powerful monarchies included **Magadha, Kosala, and Avanti**. But Vajji and Malla were different.
- However Vajji and Malla differed in terms of their administration. In these monarchies, the **assembly (sabha or samiti)** had real power. They made decisions by discussion or voting, even choosing the raja.
- These were not monarchies rather gana-sanghas—early **republics**, where many shared power. Scholars often refer to them as the world's earliest democracies.

## More Innovations
- The Age of janapadas and mahajanapadas was a period of substantial changes that shaped Indian civilisation for centuries.
- New ideas such as late **Vedic, Buddhist, and Jain** thoughts emerged. Their teachings spread through scholars, monks, and pilgrims.
- **Indian art** also grew during this time and would flourished even more in later empires.
- Urban growth also needed **new technology**. Earlier, the Harappans used **copper and bronze**, but now iron became important.
- Although ironwork had begun earlier by the **late 2nd millennium BCE**, **iron tools** gained importance — helping in **better farming** and making **stronger, sharper weapons**.
- There is also evidence of **wars between mahajanapadas**. However, we do not have an understanding of how often they happened.
- The wars, and sometimes **alliances**, resulted in the rise of **new kingdoms and empires** — which we will learn about next.
- Another big change was the **first use of coins** in India, which was needed because of growing trade.
- The first coins were made of **silver** with symbols punched onto them; these are called **punch-marked coins**. Later coins were also made from **copper, gold** and other metals.

## The Varna-Jati System
- With the continued growth of civilisation society became more complex. People were divided into different groups based on **work, class, or other features**.
- There were groups for governance, religion, education, trade, farming, crafts, etc., often each with its own role in society.
- In an ideal society, all groups should work **together in harmony**. However, in reality, these divisions often lead to **inequality**, with some groups having more **power** or wealth than others.
- In India, the society was based on two-fold system
  1. **Jati** – A group based on **profession** (like farmers, metalworkers, traders, etc.). Skills were usually passed down through generations. Each jati had its own customs, like marriage or food rules. Jatis could have **sub-jatis** as well.
  2. **Vara** – Based on ideas drawn from the **Vedas**. It comprised **four main groups**:
     - **Brahmins** – Teachers, priests, and scholars;
     - **Kshatriyas** – Warriors and rulers;
     - **Vaishyas** – Traders, farmers, and businesspeople; and
     - **Shudras** – Artisans, workers, and servants.
- During early times, people and communities in India could change their jobs if needed.
- For example, farmers might become **traders or soldiers** during a drought, and even **Brahmins** sometimes took up **trade or military work**.
- This **varna-jati** system helped organise society and give it stability. However, over time, it became **rigid**, resulting in **inequality and discrimination**, especially against lower jatis or those outside the system.
- Scholars agree that the system was more **flexible during early times**, but became stricter, particularly during British rule.
- While the **vara-jatis** system was important, it was not the only way Indian society worked — There were **many other systems**, which we will learn about later.

## Development Elsewhere in India
- During the **1st millennium BCE**, important **trade and travel routes** developed for trade, war, and pilgrimages.
- Two main routes were:
  1. **Uttarapatha** – from the **northwest** to the **Ganga plains and eastern India**.
  2. **Dakshinapatha** – from **Kaushambi** (near Prayagraj) to **South India**, crossing the **Vindhya hills**.
- These routes also connected to **ports** on the east and west coasts, which were busy trade centres.
- In the east, cities like **Sisupalgarh** (near Bhubaneswar) grew, with strong **walls and wide roads**.
- In the south, cities started appearing around **400 BCE**. Around this time, three major kingdoms rose:
  1. Cholas
  2. Cheras
  3. Pandyas
- Old **Tamil literature** presents details about the Cholas, Cheras, and Pandyas and their kings.
- South India had rich resources like **spices, gold,** and **gemstones**. Consequently, it traded with **other parts of India** and even with **foreign lands**.
- By **300–200 BCE**, almost all of India was connected — goods and ideas moved between regions and even to **Central and Southeast Asia**.
- Around this time, the mahajanapadas ended, making way for new changes that would reshape Indian history.
