## Through History
- We can learn about people's standards and ways of living, including their eating habits, clothing and types of houses they lived in.
- We can learn about the lives of people such as hunters, farmers, rulers, merchants, artisans, musicians, scientists, etc.

## How Is Time Measured in History?
- Different cultures have measured time in their own ways, often beginning new eras with significant events like a ruler's reign or a prophet's birth. Today, the Gregorian calendar is widely used globally, though traditional calendars (Hindu, Muslim, Jewish, Chinese, etc.) remain important for religious and cultural observances.
- The Gregorian system counts years from the assumed birth of Jesus Christ. Earlier, years after his birth were marked as AD (Anno Domini), now termed CE (Common Era), and years before as BC (Before Christ), now BCE (Before Common Era). For instance, 1947 CE marks India's Independence, and 560 BCE is roughly when Gautama Buddha was born.
- Timelines are useful tools in history, displaying events in sequence and helping to visualise their chronological order, such as Buddha's birth occurring before that of Jesus.

## Where Did People Live in the Past?
- Early humans, they used to wander from place to place in search of food. Many people lived on the banks of the Narmada and they were hunter-gatherers. They gathered food and knew about the vast wealth of plants in the surrounding forests. They also hunted animals.
- Around 8,000 years ago, the people of the Sulaiman and Kirthar hills in the northwest started growing crops such as wheat and barley. There was significant agricultural development in the Garo Hills as well.
- Rice was first grown by the people of the northern part of the Vindhyas.
- Apart from agriculture, they also started domesticating animals like sheep and cattle and started living in villages.
- Around 2,500 years ago, various cities were formed around the River Ganga, its tributaries and along the sea coasts.
- The southern part of the River Ganga was known as Magadha, presently within the state of Bihar. The rulers of Magadha were very powerful. They established a huge kingdom.

## How Did People Travel in the Past?
- Men and women used to travel from one part to another through hills and high mountains in search of livelihood or to escape from natural disasters such as floods or droughts.
- Merchants travelled with ships and caravans with their valuable goods.
- Many religious teachers wandered from one place to another to propagate their religious messages.
- Natural frontiers such as hills, mountains and seas were difficult to cross, but they did not become barriers for people. Many came and settled in the subcontinent.

## Names of the Land
- Our country is known by two names — India and Bharat.
- **India:** Foreigners also had their own names — Persians called it Hind or Hindu, and the Greeks called it Indos. Over time, all these names led to the name India. Today, the Constitution of India begins with the phrase "India, that is Bharat", showing how both names are still used.
- **Bharat:** Ancient Indian texts like the Rig Veda called it a Sapta Sindhava, meaning the land of seven rivers. The word Bharatavarsha, meaning the land of the Bharatas, appeared in texts like the Mahabharata. Another name was Jambudvipa, meaning the island of the jamun fruit.

## Finding Out About the Past
- **Manuscripts:** The word 'manuscripts' comes from the Latin word "Manu", meaning hand. Manuscripts are books written by hand. They were written in Sanskrit, Prakrit (the language of ordinary people) and Tamil. These books provide information about religious practices, medicines, science, etc.
- Over the years, many manuscripts have been eaten away by insects or been destroyed, but many have survived, often preserved in temples and monasteries.
- **Inscriptions:** Inscriptions are writings on relatively hard surfaces such as stone or metal. Kings got their orders inscribed so that people could see, read and obey them.
- **Archaeology:** The study of human history and prehistory through the excavation of sites and the analysis of artefacts and other physical remains.
- The inscriptions and manuscripts are studied by archaeologists. Archaeologists study and explore the remains of buildings made of stone and brick, as well as paintings and sculptures.
- Thus, there are three sources to know about the past — Manuscripts, Inscriptions and Archaeology.

## Key Words
- **Decade:** A decade is a period of 10 years.
- **Century:** A century refers to 100 years. In CE (Common Era), centuries start from 1 CE; for example, the 21st century runs from 2001 to 2100. In BCE (Before Common Era), centuries count backward; the 3rd century BCE spans from 300 to 201 BCE.
- **Millennium:** A millennium is a period of 1,000 years. The 3rd millennium CE began in 2001 and will last until 3000. Similarly, in BCE, the 1st millennium spans from 1000 to 1 BCE.

## Different People with Different Pasts
- Farmers' lives differed from those of kings and queens, and the experiences of merchants and craftsmen were distinct as well.
- Kings used to keep records of their battles and victories, whereas hunters, fishing folk, gatherers, farmers and herders did not keep many records. Therefore, much of the lives of these people remains unknown.

## Ashoka's Inscription in Kandahar
- A 2,250-year-old rock inscription was found in Kandahar, present-day Afghanistan.
- It was inscribed on the orders of a ruler named Ashoka and is written in Greek and Aramaic languages.

## What is Decipherment?
- Inscriptions contain scripts and languages. Languages and scripts have continuously transformed over time.
- Decipherment is the technique of identifying the languages and scripts inscribed on the inscriptions.

## Do You Know?
- We count dates and years from the birth of Jesus Christ.
- You must have heard about 'BC' and 'AD'. All the dates before the birth of Christ are counted backward and usually have the letters BC (Before Christ) now called the Common Era or BCE across the world.
- The years after the birth of Jesus Christ are often suffixed with the letters AD (Anno Domini), which means "in the year of the Lord (Christ)" now called Before Common Era or BCE.
