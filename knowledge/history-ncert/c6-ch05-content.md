## Introduction
Indian culture, dating back several millennia, resembles an ancient tree with deep roots and diverse branches. These branches — art, literature, science, medicine, governance, religion, and martial arts — idiom from a common cultural core. Various 'schools of thought' also developed, formed by thinkers who shared views on life and the universe.

The foundation of some of India's earliest cultural roots to the Harappan or Sindhu-Sarasvati civilisation. Over time, many philosophical traditions emerged, shaping India's distinct character. Understanding these early traditions helps us grasp the essence of India, that is Bharat.

## The Vedas
- The term *Veda* comes from the Sanskrit root *vid*, meaning knowing. There are four Vedas — Rig, Yajur, Sama, and Atharva — considered among the world's oldest texts. Composed as hymns in the Sapta Sindhava region, they were orally transmitted with remarkable precision for centuries.
- Determining the exact composition date of the Rig Veda, the oldest among the four Vedas, remains challenging, with scholars suggesting a timeline between the 5th and 2nd millennium BCE. For approximately 100 to 200 generations, these sacred texts have been meticulously memorised and transmitted orally, preserving their content with remarkable accuracy and minimal changes.
- These hymns, created by rishis and rishikas in early Sanskrit, were dedicated to deities like Indra, Agni, Varuna, Sarasvati, and others. They upheld ritam — the cosmic order. The seers viewed the divine as one, and central values like Truth were equated with the divine itself.

## Vedic Society
- Early Vedic society was structured around janas or clans — large kin-based communities.

Note: The careful oral preservation of Vedic hymns over thousand of years led to a remarkable cultural recognition — in 2008, UNESCO declared Vedic chanting as a "masterpiece of the oral and intangible heritage of humanity."

- The Rig Veda mentions over 30 such clans, like the Bharatas, Purus, Kurus, Yadus, and Turvashas, each linked to specific northwestern regions of the Indian subcontinent. While details of their governance are limited, terms like *raja* (king), *sabha*, and *samiti* suggest forms of communal assemblies.
- The Vedic texts reference a range of occupations, including farming, weaving, pottery, carpentry, healing, dancing, and priesthood, indicating a diverse and functioning society.

## Vedic Schools of Thought
- Vedic life emphasised rituals (*yajñas*), especially offerings to deities like Agni, the fire god, for personal and collective prosperity. Over time, rituals grew more elaborate, and philosophical thought expanded.
- The *Upanishads* developed deeper concepts such as karma (action and consequence), *rebirth*, and *brahman* — the ultimate divine reality pervading all existence. They also introduced *atman*, the inner Self, believed to be one with *brahman*, affirming a cosmic unity. This view fostered values of universal harmony, as seen in the prayer: *sarve bhavantu sukhinah* — "May all beings be happy."
- By the 1st millennium BCE, diverse philosophical schools emerged from Vedic roots. One such path, Yoga, offered practical methods to realise the divine within. These schools collectively laid the intellectual and spiritual foundation of what is now known as Hinduism.

## Folk and Tribal Roots
- While many aspects of Indian culture are recorded in written form, a large part survives through daily life, not writing. These include folk traditions from common people and tribal traditions from indigenous groups.
- There has always been dynamic exchange between these traditions and classical schools of thought. Deities, legends, and rituals have flowed both ways. For instance, Jagannath of Puri and various forms of the mother goddess are believed to have tribal origins. Conversely, tribal groups have long embraced Hindu deities, creating unique versions of epics like the *Mahabharata* and *Ramayana*, especially in Northeast India and Tamil Nadu.
- Nature worship is common to folk, tribal, and Hindu beliefs — mountains, rivers, trees, and animals are seen as sacred due to the divine presence within them. The Toda of Tamil Nadu, for example, revere over thirty mountain peaks. Despite a wide pantheon, many tribal groups also recognise a supreme deity. *Donyipolo* in Arunachal Pradesh, *Khandoba* in central India, and *Singbonga* among the Munda and Santhal tribes.
- Sociologist André Béteille observed that Hinduism and tribal religions have mutually influenced each other throughout history, leading to rich cultural integration.

## What is a Tribe?
- A tribe typically refers to a close-knit group of families with a shared ancestry, language, culture, and usually a common land, led by a chief. Ancient India didn't have a distinct term for tribes; they were simply janas dwelling in forests or mountains.
- Today, India's Constitution uses terms like tribes, tribal communities, and *janjati*. As of 2011, there were 705 recognised tribes, totalling over 104 million people — more than the combined population of Australia and the UK.
- Earlier, tribes were wrongly labeled as 'primitive', but modern anthropology has revealed their sophisticated and diverse cultures, challenging past stereotypes.
