## What is a Civilisation?
A civilisation is an advanced stage of human society marked by structured governance, cultural development, and technological progress. It is characterised by the establishment of cities, complex governmental systems, and an organised economy. The Harappan (or Indus Valley) Civilisation is one such early civilisation in South Asia.

## Characteristics of a Civilisation
- **Organised Government and Administration:** Civilisations had central authorities or local governing bodies to manage complex societal activities and ensure order.
- **Urbanism:** This includes planned towns and cities with wide streets, well-laid drainage, and amenities like water supply systems and drainage networks.
- **Diverse Crafts and Industries:** Civilised societies engage in specialised crafts, involving the extraction and processing of raw materials to create tools, ornaments, instruments, and other finished products.
- **Internal and External Trade:** There is active exchange of goods within the region and with distant areas, which supports economic growth and cultural interaction.
- **Writing System:** The development of writing allows for record-keeping, administration, and communication across the society.
- **Cultural Expression:** Civilisations cultivate rich traditions through art, architecture, literature, oral stories, and social practices that reflect their worldview and aesthetic values.
- **Surplus Agriculture:** Efficient farming practices produce enough food to support both rural populations and dense urban settlements.

## The Earliest Civilisation
- The most striking feature of the Harappan city is its town planning. The Harappan city was divided into two parts:
  1. **Citadel:** Placed in the west of the city. It was smaller in size but higher than the lower part.
  2. **Town:** The lower town was placed on the east and was larger in size than the citadel.
- Baked bricks were used to build walls, and were laid in an interlocking pattern that made the walls strong.
- The famous Great Bath was found in Mohenjo-Daro. Important people took a dip in this tank on special occasions. The steps leading to the tank were dipping from two sides, and there were rooms on all sides.
- Wells were found in Mohenjo-Daro, bringing water from a well and draining out after use.
- Kalibangan and Lothal had fire altars, where sacrifices may have been performed. Mohenjo-Daro, Harappa and Lothal had elaborate store-houses.
- Many of these cities had covered drains. Smaller drains were connected to bigger drains.
- Houses were either one or two storeys high, with a separate bathing area and some had wells to supply water.
- All three—houses, drains and streets—were probably planned and built at the same time.

## Life in Harappa
- **Rulers:** There is no any evidence or account about the rulers of this civilisation, perhaps a religious priests or group of elite was responsible for this.
- **Scribes:** They were the ones who knew writings and helped to prepare the seals.

## New Crafts in the City
- There were men and women, crafts people who made all kinds of things.
- Many terracotta toys have been found in Harappan cities, which show that children must have played with them.
- Most of the objects that were made and found in Harappan cities were of stone, shell and metal, including copper, bronze, gold and silver.

## The Story of Harappa
- Harappa was one of the oldest cities and the first city to be discovered in the subcontinent.
- Harappa was a modern city located in what is now Pakistan. It was first discovered by a British railway explorer who first discovered Harappa in 1826. Later on, the city was excavated by Daya Ram Sahani in 1921.
- At first, the workers thought it was the ruins of an ordinary broken city and began to use the bricks for construction projects.
- About eighty years ago, the archaeologists realised that it was not an ordinary ruin—it was one of the oldest pan-cities in the subcontinent. These cities developed about 4,700 years ago.

## Raw Materials
- Raw materials are substances that are either found naturally or produced by farmers or herders. Raw materials are processed to produce finished goods.
- Copper and bronze were used to make tools, weapons, ornaments and vessels.
- Gold and silver were used to make ornaments and vessels.
- The Harappan people made seals by using stones of rectangular shapes that had pictures of animals on them.
- The Harappans probably got copper from present-day Rajasthan and from Oman in West Asia.
- Tin, which was mixed with copper to produce bronze, was brought from present-day Afghanistan and Iran.
- Gold was brought from present-day Karnataka, and precious stones from present-day Gujarat, Iran and Afghanistan.

## Town-Planning
- The Harappan civilisation showcased advanced town planning, evident in cities like Harappa, Mohenjo-Daro, Dholavira, Rakhigarhi, and Ganweriwala.
- These cities followed precise layouts with wide, straight streets often aligned to cardinal directions. Most were fortified and divided into an upper town for elites and a lower town for commoners.
- Buildings, made of uniformly baked bricks, included homes of various sizes and public structures like warehouses. A notable example is Mohenjo-daro's Great Bath, a waterproofed tank possibly used for ritual purposes.

## Water Management
- Harappans also excelled in water management. Homes had private bathing areas connected to underground drainage systems.
- Water was sourced from wells, streams, ponds, or large reservoirs, such as those in Dholavira, where some measured up to 73 metres and were interconnected for efficient water storage and distribution. This systematic planning reflects a highly organised urban culture.

## Food Habits of the People
- Harappans grew wheat, barley, pulses, peas, rice, sesame, linseed and mustard.
- The plough was used to dig the earth for turning the soil and planting seeds.
- The water was stored and supplied to the fields when the plants were growing.
- The Harappans reared cattle, sheep, goat and buffalo, and engaged in fishing as well as hunting wild animals like antelopes.
- They also developed new tools known as ploughs, used to dig earth for planting the seeds and turning the soil.

## A Brisk Trade in the Harappan Civilisation
- The Harappan civilisation was actively involved in trade, both within its own cities and with external regions and cultures, including those beyond the Indian subcontinent. They exported items like ornaments, timber, daily-use objects, possibly gold, cotton, and some food products.
- Beads made from carnelian, a reddish semiprecious stone mostly found in Gujarat, were especially popular. Harappan artisans developed advanced methods to drill and decorate these beads. They also crafted shell bangles from conch shells, a task requiring high skill due to the shell's hardness.
- Imports likely included copper, as it was scarce in the Harappan region. Trade was conducted via land, rivers, and the sea—marking India's earliest maritime trade. Coastal settlements like Lothal had large dockyards for handling goods.
- To identify goods and traders, the Harappans used small steatite seals engraved with animal motifs and symbols, possibly linked to a writing system yet to be fully deciphered.

## The Mystery Behind the End of the Earliest Civilisation
- Around 1900 BCE, we find the beginning of a major change.
- People stopped living in many of the cities. Writings, seals and weights were no longer used.
- Raw materials brought from long distances became rare.
- In Mohenjo-Daro, it was found that garbage was piled up on the streets, the drainage system broke down and new, less impressive houses were built, even over the streets.
- The cause for the breakdown of the Indus Valley Civilisation is not yet known by anyone. Scholars have proposed different reasons for the breakdown.
- Some scholars suggest that the rivers dried up. Others suggest that there was deforestation. In some areas, there were floods. But none of these reasons can explain the end of all the cities.
- Mostly two factors are accepted. First, a climatic change which affected much of the world from 2200 BCE onwards, causing reduced rainfall and a drier phase. Second, the Sarasvat River dried up in its central basin; suddenly, cities there, such as Kalibangan or Banawall were abandoned.
