## The Empire of Ashoka
- The Maurya Empire was founded by Chandragupta Maurya with the help of Chanakya in 322 BCE. Chandragupta Maurya was the grandfather of Ashoka.
- Chanakya's ideas and teachings were written down in the famous book, Arthashastra.
- There were several cities in the empire which included the capital Pataliputra, Ujjain as well as Taxila, a gateway to the northwest, including Central Asia. Ujjain lay on the route from north to south India. Merchants, officials, and crafts-persons probably lived in these cities.
- Many forests were located in Central India, where people went in search of food. It was a vast empire and people spoke different languages, lived different lifestyles, ate various kinds of food and wore clothes various patterns.

## Difference Between Empires and Kingdoms
- Emperors need more resources than kingdoms and need to be protected by armies. Emperors also need a larger number of officials who collect taxes.
- Because the empire is so large, different regions have different ways of ruling. The area around Pataliputra was under the direct control of the emperor. Officials were appointed to collect taxes from farmers, herders, craftspersons and traders, who lived in villages and towns in the area.
- Officials also punished those who disobeyed the ruler's orders and many of these officials were given salaries.
- There were messengers and spies in the empire. The emperor supervised them all with the help of members of the royal family and senior ministers.
- There were various provinces and each of these was ruled by provincial capital such as Taxila or Ujjain.
- There were vast areas between the provincial centres, where the Mauryas tried to control roads and rivers, which were important for transport and to collect whatever resources were available as tax and tribute.
- People living in forests were usually independent but they provided elephants, timber, honey and wax to Mauryan officials.

## Megasthenes: A Greek Ambassador
- Megasthenes was an ambassador who was sent to the court of Chandragupta by the Greek ruler of West Asia named Seleucus Nicator.
- Megasthenes wrote an account of what he saw. Here is a part of his description:
  1. The occasions on which the emperor appears in public are celebrated with grand royal processions. He is carried in a golden palanquin. His guards ride elephants decorated with gold and silver.
  2. The king is normally surrounded by armed women. The king has special servants to taste the food before he eats. He never sleeps in the same bedroom for two nights.
  3. Pataliputra is a large and beautiful city. It is surrounded by a massive wall. It has 570 towers and 64 gates. The houses, which have two and three storeys, are built of wood and mud brick. The king's palace is also of wood and decorated with stone carvings. It is surrounded by gardens and enclosures for keeping birds.

## Ashoka: A Unique Ruler
- Ashoka was the most famous Mauryan ruler. He was the first ruler who tried to take his message to the people through inscriptions. Most of Ashoka's inscriptions were in Prakrit and were written in the Brahmi script.

## Rampurva: The Bull Capital
- The archaeological site is called Rampurva and is located in the West Champaran district of the border state of Bihar, situated very close to the border with Nepal.
- The lion capital is now in the Indian Museum of Kolkata.
- The Rampurva Bull was part of a Mauryan pillar found in Rampurva, Bihar and has now been placed in Rashtrapati Bhawan. It is an example of the skill of the sculptors of the time.

## Ashoka's Dhamma
- In ancient times, coastal Orissa was known as Kalinga. Ashoka fought a war so that he could conquer the great Kalinga, but remained horrified when he found out the results of the war.
- He was terrified to see all the bloodshed and the massive loss of lives in both kingdoms. Ashoka is the only king in the history of the world who gave up conquest after winning a war.

[Table: ASHOKA'S DHAMMA
- Inspiration Behind Dhamma: Ashoka was deeply inspired by the teachings of the Buddha. He believed that, like a father guiding his children, he had a duty to guide his subjects to ethical living.
- Social Problems in the Empire: Ashoka observed religious conflicts between people of different faiths and harassment of slaves and servants. He felt responsible to solve these social and moral problems.
- Dhamma Mahamatra: Ashoka appointed special officers called Dhamma Mahamatra. They job was to travel across the empire and teach people about dhamma. These officials acted as moral guides and peacekeepers.
- Communication through Edicts: Ashoka had his dhamma messages inscribed on rocks and pillars across the empire. Instructions were given to officials to read out these messages to those who couldn't read.
- Dhamma Diplomacy: Ashoka sent messengers to foreign lands such as Syria, Egypt, Greece and Sri Lanka. Their mission was to spread the message of dhamma abroad.
- Public Welfare Measures: Built roads and dug wells. Constructed rest houses for travelers. Arranged for medical treatment for both humans and animals.]
