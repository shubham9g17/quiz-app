## Introduction
- The terms and meanings of words change over time. For example, today, "Hindustan", means "India", a country, whereas in the thirteenth century, Minhaj-i-Siraj, a Persian chronicler, used the term to refer to the areas of Punjab, Haryana and the land between the Ganga and Yamuna. In the early 1500s Babur used the word Hindustan to talk about the land animals and the way people lived in the Indian subcontinent.

## Historians and their Sources
- Historians rely on coins, inscriptions, architecture and textual records for historical information. However, the significance of textual records has increased during this period (CE 700–1750).
- People used paper to write holy texts, chronicles of rulers, letters and teachings of saints, petitions and judicial records and registers of accounts and taxes.
- Manuscripts were collected by wealthy people, rulers, monasteries and temples. They were placed in libraries and archives. These manuscripts and documents provide a lot of detailed information to historians, but they are difficult to use.
- There were no printing presses, and the texts were copied by hand, leading to slight changes in them over time. It is extremely difficult to find the author's original manuscript today. We are totally dependent upon the copies made by later scribes.
- Ziyauddin Barani, a fourteenth-century chronicler, wrote his first chronicle in 1356 and a second one two years later.

## New Social and Political Groups
- The study of around 1000 years, spanning from CE 700 to 1750, is a challenge. The scale and variety of developments that occurred over the period are huge. At different moments in this period, new technologies appeared at different times in this period, like the Persian Wheel in irrigation, the spinning wheel in weaving and firearms in combat.
- New foods and beverages also arrived in the subcontinent like potatoes, corn, chillies, tea and coffee.
- All these new technologies and crops came along with people, who brought other ideas with them, thus resulting in a period of economic, political, social and cultural changes.
- Rajput's name derived from "Rajaputra", meaning, the son of a ruler. It became quite popular during this period. Between the eighth and fourteenth centuries, the term "Rajputs" was applied to a group of warriors known as Kshatriya, including not just rulers and chieftains but also soldiers and commanders who served in the armies of different monarchs all over the subcontinent. Other groups were Marathas, Sikhs, Jats, Ahoms and Kayasthas.
- Many forest-dwellers migrated during this period due to the changes in habitat. There was a gradual clearing of forests and the extension of agriculture, faster and more complete in some areas than in others.
- Peasants were required to pay taxes, and offer goods and services to local lords, such as Zamindar. As a result, a significant economic and social differences emerged among peasants.

## Caste and Jati System
- As society became more differentiated, people were grouped into jatis or sub-castes, and ranked on the basis of their backgrounds and occupations.
- Ranks were not fixed permanently and varied according to the power, influence and resources controlled by the members of the jati. The status of the same jati could vary from area to area.
- Jatis framed their own rules and regulations to manage the conduct of their members. These regulations were enforced by an assembly of elders, described in some areas as the jati panchayat.
- Jatis were also required to follow the rules of their villages. Several villages were governed by a chieftain. Together, they were only one small unit of a state.

## Region and Empires
- Around CE 700, many regions had already possessed distinct geographical dimensions and their own language and cultural characteristics.
- There was considerable conflict between these states and, occasionally, dynasties like the Cholas, Khaljis, Tughlaqs and Mughals were able to build an empire that was pan-regional, but not all these empires were equally stable or successful.
- A Sanskrit prashasti praising the Delhi Sultan Ghiyasuddin Balban (1266–87) states that he ruled over a vast empire stretching from Bengal (Gauda) in the east to Ghazni (Gajjana) in Afghanistan in the west, and it included all of South India (Dravida).
- Throughout these 1000 years from CE 700 and 1750, the character of the different regions did not grow in isolation. These regions felt the impact of larger pan-regional forces of integration without losing their distinctiveness.

## Old and New Religions
- It was during this period that important changes occurred in what we call "Hinduism" today. These included the worship of new deities, the construction of temples by royalty and the growing importance of Brahmanas, the priests, as dominant groups in society.
- One of the major developments of this period was the emergence of the idea of bhakti—a loving, personal deity that devotees could reach without the aid of priests or elaborate rituals.
- There were two beliefs in Muslims, the Shias and Sunnis. Shia believed that the Prophet Muhammad's son-in-law, Ali, was the legitimate leader of the Muslim community whereas, Sunni Muslims accepted the authority of the early leaders (Khalifas) of the community and the succeeding Khalifas.
- There were other important differences between various schools of law (Hanafi and Shafi's mainly in India) and in theology and mystic traditions.

## Important Keywords
- **Archive:** A place where documents and manuscripts are stored. Today, all national and state governments have archives where they keep their old official records and transactions.
- **Manuscript:** Manuscripts can be defined as a handwritten record of information. These are useful sources of information about the past, which could be in the form of a letter, on leaves and barks of the tree or scrolls.
- **Jati:** Jati or caste established a social hierarchy. The ranking of a person in society was determined by the caste to which he/she belonged.
- **Periodisation:** Periodisation is the process or study of categorising the past into discrete, quantified named blocks of time. This is usually done in order to facilitate the study and analysis of history, understanding the current and historical processes and causalities that might have linked those events.
- **Foreigner:** According to the medieval period, any stranger who did not belong to a certain society or culture and was not a part of that particular village was considered as a foreigner.
- **Chronicler:** A person who writes the accounts of historical events, in order of their occurrence, is called a chronicler.
