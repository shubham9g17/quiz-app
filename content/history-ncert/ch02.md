## Level 01 | Moderate

### Q1. Early humans who lived by hunting animals and gathering wild plants are called?
- (a) Pastoralists
- (b) Agriculturalists
- **(c) Hunter-gatherers** ✓
- (d) Nomadic traders
> **Explanation:** Early humans who did not grow their own food but instead hunted wild animals and collected fruits, roots, nuts, and leaves from the forest are called hunter-gatherers. This was the primary way of life for humans for most of prehistory.

### Q2. Which of the following best describes the "Palaeolithic Age"?
- (a) The age when humans first learned to grow crops
- (b) The age of bronze and iron tools
- **(c) The Old Stone Age, when humans used roughly chipped stone tools** ✓
- (d) The age when humans first built cities
> **Explanation:** The Palaeolithic Age (from Greek "palaeos" = old, "lithos" = stone) is the Old Stone Age. Humans during this period used crudely chipped stone tools for hunting and gathering. This period spans from about 2.5 million years ago to around 12,000 years ago.

### Q3. Why did early hunter-gatherers move from place to place?
- (a) They enjoyed travelling and exploring new lands
- (b) They were driven away by floods every season
- **(c) They followed animals for hunting and moved to find fresh plant food and water** ✓
- (d) They moved to avoid conflicts with neighbouring groups
> **Explanation:** Hunter-gatherers were nomadic because their survival depended on following animal herds (their main food source) and finding areas with fresh water and ripening plants. Once resources in one area were exhausted, they needed to move on.

### Q4. What was the significance of the discovery of fire for early humans?
- (a) It helped them build permanent houses
- (b) It allowed them to smelt metals like iron and copper
- **(c) It provided warmth, protection from animals, and helped in cooking food** ✓
- (d) It helped them communicate over long distances
> **Explanation:** The control of fire was one of the most important developments for early humans. Fire provided warmth in cold climates, kept predators away, and allowed humans to cook food — which made more food digestible and safer to eat, likely contributing to better health and brain development.

### Q5. Where are some of the earliest Stone Age cave paintings found in India?
- (a) Hampi, Karnataka
- (b) Sanchi, Madhya Pradesh
- **(c) Bhimbetka, Madhya Pradesh** ✓
- (d) Ajanta, Maharashtra
> **Explanation:** The Bhimbetka rock shelters in Madhya Pradesh contain some of the oldest cave paintings in India, dating back to the Stone Age. These paintings depict animals, hunting scenes, and human figures, giving us a glimpse into the life of early humans.

## Level 02 | Advanced

### Q1. Consider the following statements about the transition from hunter-gathering to agriculture:
**Statement I:** The shift to agriculture is known as the Neolithic Revolution.
**Statement II:** Agriculture first developed because hunter-gatherers ran out of animals to hunt.
Which is correct?
- **(a) Statement I is correct but Statement II is incorrect** ✓
- (b) Both Statement I and Statement II are correct
- (c) Statement I is incorrect but Statement II is correct
- (d) Both Statement I and Statement II are incorrect
> **Explanation:** The shift to settled farming, known as the Neolithic Revolution, happened around 10,000 BCE. However, it was not simply because animals ran out — it was a gradual process influenced by climate change (end of the Ice Age), increasing population, and the accidental discovery that seeds could be planted. Animals were also domesticated during this period.

### Q2. Assertion (A): Early humans preferred to live near rivers and lakes.
Reason (R): Rivers and lakes provided fresh water for drinking, attracted animals (easy prey), and supported the growth of plants that could be gathered.
- (a) A is correct but R is incorrect
- (b) Both A and R are correct, but R is not the correct explanation of A
- **(c) Both A and R are correct, and R is the correct explanation of A** ✓
- (d) A is incorrect but R is correct
> **Explanation:** Archaeological evidence shows early human settlements concentrated near water sources. Water was essential for survival — drinking, cooking, and hygiene. Animals also gathered at water bodies, making hunting easier. Additionally, water bodies supported dense vegetation that provided plant foods. R correctly explains A.

### Q3. Which of the following correctly describes a "microlith"?
- (a) A large stone axe used to cut down trees
- (b) A stone hand-axe used in the Palaeolithic Age
- **(c) A tiny, sharp stone blade used as a tool or weapon tip in the Mesolithic Age** ✓
- (d) A stone used to grind grain in early agricultural settlements
> **Explanation:** Microliths (meaning "small stones") were tiny, carefully shaped stone blades made during the Mesolithic Age (Middle Stone Age). They were hafted onto wood or bone handles to make composite tools like arrows, spears, and knives. They represent a more advanced stone-working technology than earlier Palaeolithic tools.

### Q4. The domestication of animals was a major development in early human history. Which animal is believed to have been among the FIRST to be domesticated by humans?
- (a) Horse
- (b) Cow
- **(c) Dog** ✓
- (d) Elephant
> **Explanation:** Archaeological and genetic evidence suggests dogs (domesticated from wolves) were among the first animals domesticated by humans, possibly as far back as 15,000–40,000 years ago. Dogs helped in hunting, guarding, and companionship. Sheep, goats, and cattle were domesticated later during the Neolithic period.

### Q5. In what way did the beginning of agriculture change human society most fundamentally?
- (a) Humans stopped eating meat entirely
- (b) Humans began living in boats on rivers
- **(c) Humans began to live in permanent settlements, leading to the formation of villages and social hierarchies** ✓
- (d) Humans became completely dependent on trade for food
> **Explanation:** Agriculture meant humans no longer needed to constantly move to find food. They could grow food in one place and store surpluses. This allowed permanent settlements to form, which grew into villages. Surplus food supported specialists (potters, weavers, priests), leading to social divisions and the foundation of complex societies — a fundamental transformation of human life.

## Level 03 | Previous Years

### Q1. A student is studying tools found at a prehistoric site. The tools are large, roughly chipped hand-axes made from a single stone. From which age are these tools MOST LIKELY?
- (a) Neolithic Age
- (b) Mesolithic Age
- **(c) Palaeolithic Age** ✓
- (d) Bronze Age
> **Explanation:** Large, roughly chipped stone tools (called hand-axes or choppers) are characteristic of the Palaeolithic (Old Stone Age). Mesolithic tools were smaller (microliths), Neolithic tools were polished and refined, and the Bronze Age involved metal — not stone — tools.

### Q2. Which of the following sequences correctly shows the development of human technology in prehistoric times, from EARLIEST to LATEST?
- (a) Polished stone tools → Rough stone tools → Metal tools → Fire
- **(b) Rough stone tools → Microliths → Polished stone tools → Metal tools** ✓
- (c) Fire → Metal tools → Rough stone tools → Agriculture
- (d) Agriculture → Rough stone tools → Polished stone tools → Microliths
> **Explanation:** The correct technological sequence is: rough stone tools (Palaeolithic/Old Stone Age) → microliths (Mesolithic/Middle Stone Age) → polished stone tools (Neolithic/New Stone Age) → metal tools (Chalcolithic/Bronze Age and Iron Age). This reflects increasing skill and refinement in toolmaking over time.

### Q3. What can cave paintings tell historians about early humans? Select the most complete answer.
- (a) Only the animals that early humans hunted
- (b) Only the artistic abilities of early humans
- **(c) The animals they hunted, the tools they used, their rituals or beliefs, and aspects of their daily life** ✓
- (d) The languages that early humans spoke
> **Explanation:** Cave paintings are a rich source of information about Palaeolithic and Mesolithic life. They depict animals (showing what was hunted), hunting scenes with tools and weapons, geometric patterns possibly linked to ritual or spiritual beliefs, and human figures in various activities. They reveal diet, technology, and possibly religious practices — but cannot tell us about language.

### Q4. In which of the following ways did the Neolithic Age differ from the Palaeolithic Age?
- (a) Neolithic humans were more nomadic than Palaeolithic humans
- (b) Fire was used only in the Neolithic Age and not the Palaeolithic Age
- **(c) Neolithic humans made polished stone tools and practiced agriculture, unlike Palaeolithic humans** ✓
- (d) Palaeolithic humans had a more complex social structure than Neolithic humans
> **Explanation:** The Neolithic (New Stone Age) is distinguished by two key developments: polished/ground stone tools (more refined than the chipped tools of the Palaeolithic) and the practice of agriculture and animal domestication. Neolithic humans were more settled. Fire was likely used in both ages. Palaeolithic social structures were simpler, not more complex.

### Q5. Early humans are believed to have migrated out of Africa to other parts of the world. What is the primary evidence historians and scientists use to support this "Out of Africa" theory?
- (a) Written records found in ancient African manuscripts
- (b) Cave paintings found only in Africa depicting migration routes
- **(c) Fossil remains, genetic (DNA) evidence, and the distribution of stone tools across continents** ✓
- (d) Oral traditions passed down by African communities for thousands of years
> **Explanation:** The "Out of Africa" theory is supported by multiple lines of scientific evidence: fossil remains of early Homo sapiens found earliest and in greatest variety in Africa; genetic studies showing all modern humans share a common African ancestral population; and the pattern of stone tool types found across Africa, the Middle East, Asia, and Europe that tracks migration routes. Written records did not exist during this prehistoric period.
