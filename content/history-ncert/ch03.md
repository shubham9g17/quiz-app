## Level 01 | Moderate

### Q1. The Harappan Civilisation is also known by which other name?
- (a) Vedic Civilisation
- (b) Gangetic Civilisation
- **(c) Indus Valley Civilisation** ✓
- (d) Deccan Civilisation
> **Explanation:** The Harappan Civilisation is also called the Indus Valley Civilisation because many of its important cities — including Mohenjo-Daro and Harappa — were located along the Indus River and its tributaries. It is one of the world's earliest urban civilisations, flourishing around 2600–1900 BCE.

### Q2. The Great Bath, one of the most remarkable structures of the Harappan period, was found at which site?
- (a) Harappa
- (b) Lothal
- **(c) Mohenjo-Daro** ✓
- (d) Kalibangan
> **Explanation:** The Great Bath is located at Mohenjo-Daro in present-day Pakistan. It is a large, watertight tank made of baked bricks and is considered one of the earliest public water tanks in the ancient world. Historians believe it may have been used for ritual bathing or public cleansing.

### Q3. Which of the following best describes the drainage system of Harappan cities?
- (a) Waste water was directed into rivers without any planning
- (b) Each house had a separate well to dispose of waste
- **(c) An advanced underground drainage system connected individual homes to city drains** ✓
- (d) Drainage was managed by pouring waste into large open pits outside the city
> **Explanation:** One of the most impressive features of Harappan urban planning was its sophisticated drainage system. Covered drains ran beneath the streets, connecting house drains to larger city drains. This level of urban sanitation was remarkable for its time and far ahead of many contemporary civilisations.

### Q4. Lothal, an important Harappan site, is located in which present-day Indian state?
- (a) Rajasthan
- (b) Punjab
- **(c) Gujarat** ✓
- (d) Uttar Pradesh
> **Explanation:** Lothal is located near the Gulf of Khambhat in present-day Gujarat, India. It is famous for its well-planned dockyard, which suggests it was an important centre of maritime trade for the Harappan Civilisation.

### Q5. Harappan cities were typically divided into two distinct parts. What were these called?
- (a) Inner city and outer city
- (b) Market town and temple town
- **(c) The Citadel (upper town) and the Lower Town** ✓
- (d) The Royal Quarter and the Common Quarter
> **Explanation:** Harappan cities had a clear two-part layout. The Citadel (or upper town) was built on a raised platform and likely housed important public buildings, granaries, and possibly the ruling elite. The Lower Town was larger and densely packed with residential buildings for the general population.

## Level 02 | Advanced

### Q1. Consider the following statements about the Harappan script:
**Statement I:** The Harappan script has been fully deciphered by modern archaeologists.
**Statement II:** The Harappan script was written from right to left.
Which is correct?
- (a) Both Statement I and Statement II are correct
- (b) Statement I is correct but Statement II is incorrect
- **(c) Statement I is incorrect but Statement II is correct** ✓
- (d) Both Statement I and Statement II are incorrect
> **Explanation:** The Harappan (Indus) script remains undeciphered to this day — Statement I is incorrect. However, it has been observed that the script was generally written from right to left (and sometimes in a boustrophedon style on longer texts) — Statement II is correct. The inability to read this script is one reason why so much about Harappan society remains unknown.

### Q2. Assertion (A): Historians believe Harappan cities had a well-organised civic administration.
Reason (R): Evidence of uniform brick sizes, standardised weights and measures, and a planned street grid across geographically distant Harappan sites suggests centralised planning and regulation.
- (a) A is correct but R is incorrect
- (b) Both A and R are correct, but R is not the correct explanation of A
- **(c) Both A and R are correct, and R is the correct explanation of A** ✓
- (d) A is incorrect but R is correct
> **Explanation:** The remarkable standardisation seen across Harappan sites — identical brick ratios (1:2:4), uniform weights and measures, and similarly planned street layouts — strongly suggests some form of centralised authority that enforced these standards across a vast geographical area. This is used as the primary evidence for organised civic administration.

### Q3. Which of the following is NOT typically associated with the Harappan Civilisation?
- (a) Planned streets in a grid pattern
- (b) Use of baked bricks in construction
- **(c) Construction of large temples and pyramids** ✓
- (d) A sophisticated underground drainage system
> **Explanation:** Unlike Mesopotamian or Egyptian civilisations, the Harappan Civilisation shows no evidence of large, monumental temples or pyramid-like structures. While the Great Bath may have had a ritual purpose, there are no grand temples comparable to those found in contemporary civilisations. Grid-planned streets, baked bricks, and drainage systems are all distinctively Harappan.

### Q4. The dockyard found at Lothal is considered evidence of which aspect of Harappan society?
- (a) Advanced military power and naval warfare
- (b) Religious rituals involving water
- **(c) Active maritime trade with other civilisations** ✓
- (d) Flood control and water management for agriculture
> **Explanation:** The dockyard at Lothal, with its connection to the sea via an estuary, is interpreted by archaeologists as a facility for loading and unloading trade goods from ships. Harappan artefacts found in Mesopotamia (modern Iraq) and Mesopotamian goods found in Harappan sites confirm that active long-distance maritime trade existed.

### Q5. Which of the following provides the strongest evidence that trade existed between the Harappan Civilisation and Mesopotamia?
- (a) Similarities in the languages of the two civilisations
- (b) Identical styles of pottery found at both locations
- **(c) Harappan seals found in Mesopotamian sites and Mesopotamian lapis lazuli beads found at Harappan sites** ✓
- (d) Written trade agreements discovered at both Harappa and Ur
> **Explanation:** Archaeological evidence of trade between the Harappan Civilisation and Mesopotamia includes Harappan square seals (with bull and unicorn motifs) found at Mesopotamian sites like Ur and Kish, and goods like lapis lazuli (sourced from modern Afghanistan/Iran) found at Harappan sites. These physical objects are the strongest evidence of exchange between the two civilisations.

## Level 03 | Previous Years

### Q1. The Harappan Civilisation is considered one of the world's first "urban" civilisations. Which combination of features BEST justifies this description?
- (a) Large population, use of iron, and the invention of writing
- (b) Monumental architecture, a royal family, and use of the wheel
- **(c) Planned street layout, specialised occupations, a drainage system, and trade networks** ✓
- (d) Agriculture, animal domestication, and the use of baked bricks
> **Explanation:** Urban civilisations are characterised by features beyond simple farming: planned city layouts (streets in a grid), social specialisation (craftspeople, traders, administrators), public infrastructure (drainage), and trade with distant regions. Agriculture and animal domestication were present in village cultures before cities appeared. The combination in option (c) best defines what makes a society "urban."

### Q2. Harappan sites have been found across a very large geographical area — from Sutkagendor in Balochistan to Alamgirpur in Uttar Pradesh. What does this wide distribution suggest?
- (a) The Harappan people were nomadic and did not build permanent cities
- (b) The Harappan Civilisation was actually several unrelated cultures
- **(c) The Harappan Civilisation was one of the largest early civilisations in terms of geographical extent** ✓
- (d) The Harappan people regularly relocated their cities every few decades
> **Explanation:** The Harappan Civilisation, at its peak, covered approximately 1.5 million square kilometres — making it the largest of the three major early civilisations (the others being Egypt and Mesopotamia) in terms of geographical spread. The consistent material culture (similar pottery, bricks, weights) across this vast area indicates a unified civilisation, not unrelated cultures.

### Q3. Around 1900 BCE, the Harappan Civilisation went into decline. Which of the following is among the theories historians propose to explain this decline?
- (a) The Harappans were conquered by the Romans
- (b) A massive volcanic eruption destroyed all Harappan cities simultaneously
- **(c) Climate change causing reduced rainfall, leading to drought and agricultural failure** ✓
- (d) The Harappans voluntarily abandoned cities to return to a hunter-gatherer lifestyle
> **Explanation:** The decline of the Harappan Civilisation around 1900–1700 BCE is still debated by historians. Among the most widely supported theories are: climate change that caused prolonged drought; shifts in river courses (especially the drying up of the Ghaggar-Hakra river); and possibly flooding. There is no evidence of Roman contact (Rome didn't exist yet) or a single volcanic event destroying all cities simultaneously.

### Q4. A student notices that bricks used at Mohenjo-Daro, Harappa, and Lothal — cities hundreds of kilometres apart — have the same ratio of length to width to height (1:2:4). What is the MOST significant historical inference from this observation?
- (a) The Harappans had access to the same type of clay at all sites
- (b) Harappan builders copied each other's designs by coincidence
- **(c) There was likely some form of central authority or shared standards that regulated construction across the civilisation** ✓
- (d) All Harappan cities were built by the same group of travelling builders
> **Explanation:** Standardised brick sizes across distant sites is one of the strongest arguments for centralised planning or at least a shared set of technical standards in the Harappan Civilisation. Such uniformity over a vast geographical area is unlikely to be coincidental and suggests some form of coordinated regulation — whether through a central government, a system of guilds, or widely accepted conventions.

### Q5. The Harappan Civilisation left behind no surviving royal tombs, grand palaces, or monuments to rulers, unlike ancient Egypt or Mesopotamia. What does this absence MOST likely indicate about Harappan society?
- (a) The Harappans had no rulers or leaders of any kind
- (b) All royal monuments were destroyed in the decline of the civilisation
- **(c) Power in Harappan society may have been organised differently — possibly through merchant or priestly councils rather than a single powerful king** ✓
- (d) Harappan rulers were too poor to build monuments
> **Explanation:** The near-complete absence of royal imagery, grand tombs, or palaces distinguishing elite residences from common ones is striking when compared to Egypt or Mesopotamia, where pharaohs and kings left abundant evidence of their power. This has led historians to suggest that Harappan society may have been organised around collective leadership — perhaps by wealthy merchants or religious leaders — rather than a single god-king. The civilisation was clearly not poor, given its infrastructure and trade goods.
